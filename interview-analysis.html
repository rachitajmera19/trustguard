Workshop · Interview Analysis
Cyberbullying & Online Fraud in India · May 2026
Team:
Divyam Bafna · Manya Goyal
Rishabh Chaturvedi · Tanishka Sirohi
Rachit Ajmera · Naitik Gaur
Harshit Brar · Harshit Golani
Common Problems Found
Lack of Awareness
Not knowing what is safe online — messages and calls
Lack of knowledge about cyber risks and digital safety
Elderly are more vulnerable due to lack of knowledge — hard to tell genuine from fake
People don't report often — it is harsh, time-consuming, and there is no trust in the system
Schools are not doing enough — majority treat it superficially, the problem is ignored and undervalued
The Average Persona
Digitally influenced, slightly tech-aware user
Usually informed but has a fundamental lack of cyber-safety awareness
They lack a reliable system — doesn't know who to call or where to go
Victim to manipulation and social engineering tactics
Highly likely to feel helpless; seeks simple, fast solutions
Common Solutions Offered
Awareness campaigns
Education on basic digital safety habits
Educating people by organising workshops
Alertness to common fraud topics
Spreading awareness through social media
Helping people by expanding digital literacy
Unique Problem Statements
Consumer Responsibility — users themselves are responsible; internet safety is a personal responsibility, not just a system-level issue
Absence of proactive information systems for banning and harassment prevention
Work identity notification missing in online platforms
Normalisation of cyberbullying in online culture — not seen as a serious crime
Emotional understanding lacking during cyber incidents — no empathetic support
Privacy anxiety due to personal data exposure and awareness of data collection
Real-time anti-fraud notification system is absent — no alerts during suspicious transactions
Dark-side real-time information on platforms — harmful content spreads faster than moderation
No mandatory phone number tracking for harassers — anonymity enables repeat abuse
Cultural barriers in accessing safety UX — language and design exclusions
Localised criminal networks operating through digital channels go untracked
Planned and coordinated criminal activities not detected by current systems
Accountability gap — poor digital design that doesn't factor in misuse scenarios
Legal & communal criminal network links missing in reporting systems
Unique Solutions
Consumer responsibility platform — users can flag, rate, and report digital threats collectively
Proactive information system with real-time banning protocols
Work identity verification layer on online platforms to reduce anonymity
Normalisation counter-campaigns — educate that cyberbullying is a cognisable offence
Emotional support chatbot integrated into fraud reporting flow
Privacy data audit tool to show users who has their data
Real-time anti-fraud notification integrated with UPI and banking APIs
Content velocity detection — flag posts spreading abnormally fast for human review
Mandatory phone number linking for social media accounts with harassment history
Localised criminal network map accessible to law enforcement agencies
Coordinated attack detection using graph-based analysis of account behaviour
Platform accountability score — public dashboard rating platforms on safety response speed
Legal cross-referencing tool linking multiple digital crime reports automatically
Learnings
Awareness exists but is superficial — not deep enough to change behaviour under pressure
Human tendency to trust family/authority (social grade) is the main cause of scams succeeding
Human literacy about digital fraud is the real gap — not technological barriers
Scams are highly relational — they target social bonds, not just technical vulnerabilities
Reporting is too slow — needs 4+ steps to activate any official response
Schools and colleges don't provide practical safety education — only theoretical awareness
Cyberbullying is a cultural phenomenon, not a system design failure alone — requires social change alongside design
Safety depends on lived experience, not just awareness campaigns
Current approach is reactionary, not preventive — systems respond after harm, not before
→ These learnings directly shaped our HMW selection and prototype direction
What It Means

Key insights extracted

The workshop synthesis surfaced patterns that directly shaped our design direction. Here are the five most consequential insights.

Insight 01
Awareness ≠ Behaviour Change
Participants were aware of digital risks but still fell victim. Knowledge doesn't translate to safety under pressure, urgency, or emotional manipulation. Design must work even when users are in a panicked or emotionally compromised state.
Insight 02
Trust Is the Attack Vector
The primary mechanism of every scam described was exploiting social trust — family bonds, authority figures, urgency. Scammers don't hack systems; they hack human psychology. Design must build counter-trust signals that are visible even in high-stress moments.
Insight 03
Speed Is the Design Problem
Current reporting systems require 4+ steps and hours to activate any meaningful response. Scammers move money in minutes. The design must close the gap between harm occurring and institutional response — measured in seconds, not hours.
Insight 04
Institutional Failure Is Universal
Schools, banks, police, and platforms all received zero trust ratings in our workshop. Not a single participant felt any institution was proactively protecting them. Any design solution must work independently of institutional trust — with embedded legal mechanisms built-in.
Insight 05
Prevention, Not Just Reaction
Every existing solution in India is reactive — report after the fact, freeze after the transfer, arrest after the harm. The workshop consensus: the most impactful design would be one that intervenes before or during the moment of harm, not 24 hours later.
Insight 06
Design Excludes the Most Vulnerable
The people most harmed — elderly users, low-literacy users, regional language speakers — are the least served by existing digital safety tools. Every safety product is designed for educated, English-speaking urban users. Radical accessibility is not a nice-to-have; it's the core design challenge.
Connection to Project

How this shaped everything

This workshop was the pivotal synthesis moment in our project — the point where raw interview data became actionable design direction. The four quadrants on the chart paper map directly onto our project structure.

P
Common Problems → Root Cause Analysis
The problem quadrant became the foundation for our 10-theme RCA and 118 HMW questions in the Define phase.
S
Unique Problem Statements → HMW Selection
The 14 unique problem statements fed directly into the HMW shortlist — the top-ranked one becoming our selected design challenge.
I
Unique Solutions → Ideation Seed Ideas
The solution quadrant seeded the 6-3-5 Brainwriting workshop, with several of these ideas evolving into our 7 final concepts.
L
Learnings → Design Principles
The learnings — especially "reactionary not preventive" and "speed is the design problem" — became the core principles guiding TrustGuard's architecture.
Workshop → Outcome Mapping
"Reactionary" → TrustGuard's lock-screen SOS: intervene in the first 120 seconds
"Trust attack" → Visible 256-bit security badge: visual counter-trust signal
"4+ steps" → 5-field form, 90-second completion, one shareable ID
"No system" → Universal Fraud-Event ID that works across banks + police
"Excludes elderly" → Voice-first, dialect-aware confirmation layer for Persona 02
