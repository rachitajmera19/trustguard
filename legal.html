Legal Framework

The Indian legal ecosystem governing digital harm — spanning the Information Technology Act 2000, the Indian Penal Code, the IT Amendment Act 2008, and the Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023. Each provision is written in full below, with its applicability to our project explained.

Act 01 of 04

Information Technology Act, 2000

India's primary legislation governing cybercrimes and electronic transactions. Enacted on 17 October 2000, it was India's first law to deal with cybercrime and e-commerce. The Act has been significantly amended — most importantly by the IT Amendment Act of 2008.

IT Act · 2000  |  Parliament of India
Information Technology Act, 2000
Enacted 17 October 2000  ·  Amended 2008  ·  90 Sections
In Force
Section 66A
Punishment for Sending Offensive Messages through Communication Service
Information Technology Act, 2000 — Section 66A (now struck down)
This section made it a punishable offence — up to three years imprisonment and a fine — to send any information through a computer or communication device that was grossly offensive, menacing, or known to be false but sent for the purpose of causing annoyance, inconvenience, danger, obstruction, insult, injury, criminal intimidation, enmity, hatred, or ill will.

Why it mattered: Section 66A was the most direct provision against online harassment and cyberbullying. It was used in cases involving threatening messages, fake social media profiles created to defame, and coordinated trolling campaigns.
Struck down by Supreme Court: In Shreya Singhal vs. Union of India (2015), the Supreme Court of India struck down Section 66A in its entirety on grounds that it violated Article 19(1)(a) of the Constitution — the fundamental right to freedom of speech and expression. The provision was found to be vague, overbroad, and prone to misuse. Despite this, it remains historically critical because many early cyberbullying FIRs were filed under it, and its removal left a legislative gap that has not been filled.
Struck down — Shreya Singhal vs. UOI, 2015 Legislative gap remains
Section 66C
Punishment for Identity Theft
Information Technology Act, 2000 — Section 66C
Whoever, fraudulently or dishonestly makes use of the electronic signature, password, or any other unique identification feature of any other person shall be punished with imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to three years and shall also be liable to fine which may extend to one lakh rupees.

Why it matters for this project: UPI fraud and phone-based scams almost always involve the fraudster obtaining the victim's OTP, PIN, or account credentials — either by deception or by impersonation. The moment a scammer uses a victim's OTP to authorise a transaction, Section 66C is triggered. This section forms the legal backbone for prosecuting the majority of digital financial fraud cases in India.
Imprisonment up to 3 years Fine up to Rs. 1,00,000 Covers OTP theft & UPI fraud
Section 66D
Punishment for Cheating by Personation Using Computer Resource
Information Technology Act, 2000 — Section 66D
Whoever, by means of any communication device or computer resource cheats by personating, shall be punished with imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to three years and shall also be liable to fine which may extend to one lakh rupees.

Why it matters for this project: The "digital arrest" scam described in our research — where fraudsters impersonated CBI officers, TRAI officials, or bank representatives over video calls and phone — is a direct violation of Section 66D. It is also applicable when scammers create fake customer-care profiles, impersonate bank helplines on social media, or use spoofed caller IDs to appear as official institutions.
Imprisonment up to 3 years Fine up to Rs. 1,00,000 Covers fake official calls & digital arrest scams
Section 67
Punishment for Publishing or Transmitting Obscene Material in Electronic Form
Information Technology Act, 2000 — Section 67
Whoever publishes or transmits or causes to be published or transmitted in the electronic form, any material which is lascivious or appeals to the prurient interest or if its effect is such as to tend to deprave and corrupt persons who are likely, having regard to all relevant circumstances, to read, see or hear the matter contained or embodied in it, shall be punished with imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to three years and with fine which may extend to five lakh rupees on first conviction.

On conviction of a second or subsequent offence, imprisonment may extend to five years and fine up to ten lakh rupees.

Why it matters for this project: This section directly applies to the obscene and sexually abusive content posted against Renu Desai. It also applies to the harassment Shanaya Khan faced — where the comments were sexually explicit and targeted at a 14-year-old. Section 67 was one of the provisions used by Cyberabad Police when formally registering the case.
Up to 3 years (1st offence), up to 5 years (repeat) Fine up to Rs. 5 lakh / Rs. 10 lakh Applied in Renu Desai case, 2025
Act 02 of 04

Information Technology Amendment Act, 2008

Passed on 23 December 2008, this amendment significantly expanded the IT Act to address emerging cyber threats including data theft, identity fraud, and platform liability. It introduced several new sections — most critically Section 43A and Section 66C through 66F.

IT Amendment Act · 2008  |  Parliament of India
Information Technology (Amendment) Act, 2008
Enacted 5 February 2009  ·  Amends IT Act 2000  ·  Introduced 22 new sections
In Force
Section 43A
Compensation for Failure to Protect Sensitive Personal Data or Information
IT Amendment Act, 2008 — Section 43A
Where a body corporate, possessing, dealing or handling any sensitive personal data or information in a computer resource which it owns, controls or operates, is negligent in implementing and maintaining reasonable security practices and procedures and thereby causes wrongful loss or wrongful gain to any person, such body corporate shall be liable to pay damages by way of compensation not exceeding five crore rupees to the person so affected.

Why it matters for this project: Banks, UPI platforms (NPCI, PhonePe, Google Pay), and social media platforms all handle sensitive financial and personal data. If their security negligence enables fraud or harassment — for example, failing to implement two-factor authentication, or allowing anonymous accounts to mass-report a user — they can be held liable under Section 43A. This provision creates the legal basis for compelling platforms to invest in proactive safety infrastructure rather than treating it as optional.
Compensation up to Rs. 5 crore Applies to corporates / platforms Covers data negligence enabling fraud
Section 66E
Punishment for Violation of Privacy
IT Amendment Act, 2008 — Section 66E
Whoever intentionally or knowingly captures, publishes or transmits the image of a private area of any person without his or her consent, under circumstances violating the privacy of that person, shall be punished with imprisonment which may extend to three years or with fine not exceeding two lakh rupees, or with both.

Why it matters for this project: This section is directly applicable to image-based sexual abuse (IBSA) — the creation and sharing of intimate images without consent. It is also relevant in cases where screenshots of private conversations are shared publicly to defame or harass someone, and in the doxxing of victims where private photographs are circulated.
Imprisonment up to 3 years Fine up to Rs. 2 lakh Covers non-consensual image sharing
Act 03 of 04

Indian Penal Code (IPC)

The Indian Penal Code, enacted in 1860, predates the internet — but several of its provisions are regularly applied to online offences because the underlying acts of harassment, intimidation, defamation, and abuse remain criminalised regardless of the medium through which they occur.

IPC · 1860  |  Parliament of India
Indian Penal Code, 1860
Enacted 1860  ·  Amended multiple times  ·  Being replaced by Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023
Being replaced
Section 354A
Sexual Harassment and Punishment for Sexual Harassment
Indian Penal Code — Section 354A (inserted by Criminal Law Amendment Act, 2013)
A man committing any of the following acts shall be guilty of the offence of sexual harassment —

(i) physical contact and advances involving unwelcome and explicit sexual overtures; or (ii) a demand or request for sexual favours; or (iii) showing pornography against the will of a woman; or (iv) making sexually coloured remarks, shall be guilty of the offence of sexual harassment.

Punishment: The offences under clauses (i), (ii) and (iii) shall be punishable with rigorous imprisonment for a term which may extend to three years, or with fine, or with both. The offence under clause (iv) — making sexually coloured remarks — shall be punishable with imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to one year, or with fine, or with both.

Why it matters for this project: Section 354A is directly applicable to the sexually abusive language directed at Renu Desai and the sexualised comments directed at Shanaya Khan. The section was specifically inserted following the 2012 Nirbhaya case to explicitly criminalise sexual harassment in all its forms — including verbal and written harassment through online platforms.
Up to 3 years (clauses i–iii), up to 1 year (clause iv) Applied alongside IT Act in Renu Desai case
Section 354D
Stalking
Indian Penal Code — Section 354D (inserted by Criminal Law Amendment Act, 2013)
Any man who — (i) follows a woman and contacts, or attempts to contact such woman to foster personal interaction repeatedly despite a clear indication of disinterest by such woman; or (ii) monitors the use by a woman of the internet, email or any other form of electronic communication, commits the offence of stalking.

Punishment: On first conviction, imprisonment for a term which may extend to three years and shall also be liable to fine. On a second or subsequent conviction, imprisonment for a term which may extend to five years and shall also be liable to fine.

Why it matters for this project: Section 354D is the most direct provision against cyberstalking. It explicitly includes monitoring a woman's internet and email use, meaning any man who tracks a victim's social media activity, checks-in obsessively on their location, or monitors their online posts against their will is committing a cognisable offence. This is the legal basis for protecting victims like Shanaya Khan from continued contact after she blocked the harassers.
Up to 3 years (1st offence), up to 5 years (repeat) Explicitly covers cyberstalking and email monitoring
Section 499
Defamation
Indian Penal Code — Section 499
Whoever, by words either spoken or intended to be read, or by signs or by visible representations, makes or publishes any imputation concerning any person intending to harm, or knowing or having reason to believe that such imputation will harm, the reputation of such person, is said, except in the cases hereinafter excepted, to defame that person.

Punishment: Under Section 500 IPC — simple imprisonment for a term which may extend to two years, or with fine, or with both.

Why it matters for this project: The deliberate spread of false propaganda against Renu Desai — including fabricated claims about her personal life and professional conduct — constitutes defamation under Section 499. Online defamation through social media posts, fake screenshots, and morphed images is covered under this section read with the IT Act.
Imprisonment up to 2 years (under Section 500) Covers false propaganda, fake screenshots
Section 509
Word, Gesture or Act Intended to Insult the Modesty of a Woman
Indian Penal Code — Section 509
Whoever, intending to insult the modesty of any woman, utters any word, makes any sound or gesture, or exhibits any object, intending that such word or sound shall be heard, or that such gesture or object shall be seen, by such woman, or intrudes upon the privacy of such woman, shall be punished with simple imprisonment for a term which may extend to three years, and also with fine.

Why it matters for this project: Section 509 is one of the oldest but still actively used provisions against gendered online abuse. In the Renu Desai case, the obscene and vulgar language used in posts specifically targeting her as a woman falls under this section. The Cyberabad Police applied it alongside Section 67 of the IT Act to provide a more comprehensive legal basis for arrest and prosecution.
Simple imprisonment up to 3 years Applied in Renu Desai case alongside IT Act Section 67
"The IPC provisions, while pre-digital, are not obsolete. Courts have consistently held that the medium of a crime does not change its character — harassment through a smartphone is still harassment under Section 354A."
— General principle of Indian cyber law jurisprudence
Act 04 of 04

Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023

India's first dedicated personal data protection law, passed on 11 August 2023. The DPDP Act is foundational for any design solution that processes victim data — including the cross-bank freeze system proposed in our project. Its provisions govern how data may be collected, used, shared, and deleted.

DPDP Act · 2023  |  Parliament of India
Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023
Enacted 11 August 2023  ·  Rules under consultation  ·  44 Sections
Partially notified
Section 8
General Obligations of a Data Fiduciary
DPDP Act, 2023 — Section 8
A Data Fiduciary — any entity that determines the purpose and means of processing personal data — must ensure the completeness, accuracy, and consistency of personal data. It must implement appropriate technical and organisational measures to ensure effective observance of the provisions of this Act and the rules made thereunder.

Data Fiduciaries must not retain personal data beyond the period necessary for the purpose for which it was collected. They must make reasonable efforts to ensure that personal data is accurate and complete, especially if it is likely to be used to make a decision that affects the Data Principal (the individual whose data is being processed).

Why it matters for this project: Banks, NPCI, and the National Cybercrime Portal are all Data Fiduciaries when they process fraud reports. Under Section 8, they are legally obligated to keep victim data accurate, secure, and deleted once the case is resolved. Our TrustGuard system must be designed as a compliant Data Fiduciary — with transparent data retention policies and automatic deletion of reports after case closure.
Applies to all entities processing victim data Mandates technical security measures
Section 9
Processing of Personal Data of Children
DPDP Act, 2023 — Section 9
A Data Fiduciary shall, before processing any personal data of a child, obtain verifiable consent of the parent or the lawful guardian of such child. A Data Fiduciary shall not undertake processing of personal data that is likely to cause any detrimental effect on the well-being of a child. A Data Fiduciary shall not undertake tracking or behavioural monitoring of children.

Why it matters for this project: This section is directly relevant to the Shanaya Khan case — a 14-year-old victim. Any reporting system used by a minor must obtain parental consent for data processing. Critically, social media platforms that track, profile, and target content at minors — enabling harassment to reach them at scale — may be in violation of Section 9.
Requires parental consent for under-18 data Prohibits behavioural tracking of children
Section 11
Consent
DPDP Act, 2023 — Section 11
Every Data Fiduciary shall give the Data Principal the option to withdraw consent at any time, with the ease of such withdrawal being comparable to the ease with which consent was given. Upon withdrawal of consent, the Data Fiduciary shall, within a reasonable time, cease to process the personal data of such Data Principal and cause its Data Processors to do the same.

Why it matters for this project: Victims who file a fraud report through TrustGuard or any similar system must have the ability to withdraw their data at any point — for example, if they do not wish to proceed with legal action, or if the matter is resolved out of court. The system must implement a clear, one-tap withdrawal mechanism that is no harder to use than the original consent form.
Right to withdraw consent at any time Withdrawal triggers data deletion obligation
Enforcement Precedent

The Telangana zero-tolerance model

The Renu Desai case in 2025 established a clear precedent in Telangana: digital forensics can pierce online anonymity, and law enforcement will act — every time. Cyberabad Cybercrime Police deployed specialised forensic tools to trace the four accounts responsible for the harassment campaign, resulting in two arrests within weeks of the formal complaint.

This is significant for design. The legal gap is not one of will — courts and police in Telangana have demonstrated willingness to act. The gap is one of access and speed — most victims do not have the platform, resources, or knowledge to navigate the complaint process that Renu Desai was able to activate.

"Online anonymity is no shield from the law."
— Cyberabad Cybercrime Police, following the Renu Desai case, 2025

Key Lessons for Design
1
Law Enforcement Will Act When Evidence Is Presented
The Telangana precedent proves that properly documented evidence leads to prosecution. TrustGuard's Fraud-Event ID system creates exactly this kind of structured, accessible documentation.
2
Speed Remains the Critical Design Gap
Even in the most favourable enforcement environment, the process took weeks. In financial fraud, money moves in minutes. The design must compress the response window — not just make it easier to report, but trigger action in real time.
3
Existing Laws Have Not Kept Pace with Technology
Section 66A being struck down left a gap. The IPC was written in 1860. Even the DPDP Act 2023 has not yet had its rules fully notified. Dedicated cyberbullying legislation with faster response timelines is still needed.
4
Enforcement Is Inconsistent Across States
Telangana's zero-tolerance approach is not standard across India. The design solution must work in weak-enforcement environments too — not rely on strong-enforcement as a prerequisite.
5
Victims Should Not Bear the Reporting Burden
Renu Desai had to publicly announce legal action herself to trigger a response. The burden fell on the victim — not the platform, not the police. Design must shift this burden onto the system.
