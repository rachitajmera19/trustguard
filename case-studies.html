Real-World Context

Case Studies

Two documented real-world cases of cyberbullying in India that shaped every design decision — revealing systemic failures in platforms, law enforcement, and support systems.

Overview

Real cases, real consequences

Beyond interviews, we analysed two documented cases of cyberbullying in India. Each case reveals systemic failures and provides concrete evidence for specific design decisions in our solution.

SK
Shanaya Khan
14 years · Hyderabad · Teen Cyberbullying
What Happened

Shanaya posted a harmless photo with her best friend and immediately received homophobic slurs and abusive comments from anonymous users. Even after deleting the post, abusive messages continued arriving directly in her DMs from strangers she had never met.

Contributing Factors

Easy access to smartphones and social media without supervision. Misuse of anonymous messaging features. Exposure to harmful online communities that coordinate targeted harassment. No platform mechanism to prevent continued contact after blocking.

Psychological Impact

Mental health deteriorated significantly. Shanaya confided in her elder sister rather than parents or school authorities — illustrating the shame-driven silence that surrounds teen cyberbullying. Her sister arranged counselling. Teachers at her school reported frequent "online wars" among students.

Expert Observations

Psychiatrists note that girls are particularly vulnerable to sexualised online harassment. Schools began warning parents about unrestricted internet access and running workshops on responsible digital monitoring.

Design Insight

Young victims need trauma-informed, private reporting tools and peer support mechanisms — not just a buried "report" button. They also need systems that prevent perpetrators from accessing them across platforms after being blocked once.

RD
Renu Desai
Actress · Hyderabad · Coordinated Harassment
What Happened

Four coordinated social media accounts subjected Renu Desai to sustained harassment using vulgar and sexually abusive language. The abuse intensified every time she expressed public opinions on social issues — a deliberate silencing tactic designed to deter women from speaking publicly.

Nature of the Abuse

Obscene comments and false propaganda from four accounts acting in unison — suggesting organised, coordinated targeting. The abuse was trigger-based: her views on street dogs became a specific flashpoint for a coordinated trolling campaign.

Investigation & Outcome

Desai formally reported four accounts to Cyberabad Cybercrime Police. Investigators deployed digital forensic tools to trace the anonymous accounts. Two suspects were identified and arrested under the IT Act and IPC. The remaining two are actively sought. Case registered formally.

Police Warning
"Online anonymity is no shield from the law."
— Cyberabad Cybercrime Police, 2025
Design Insight

Victims should not bear the burden of legal action. The fact that Desai had to publicly announce legal action herself exposes the inadequacy of platform-level moderation. Platforms need proactive, automated detection of coordinated harassment — not just reactive reporting.

Cross-Case Analysis

Patterns across both cases

Anonymity Enables Harm
Both cases involved anonymous or pseudonymous perpetrators. Digital forensics can pierce this anonymity — but only after the fact. Design must reduce the harm window, not just enable eventual identification.
Platforms Took No Action
In both cases, platforms failed to intervene proactively. Victims had to take action themselves — public announcements, police complaints, counselling — carrying the burden that should fall on the system.
Psychological Harm is Primary
In both cases, emotional and psychological damage was the primary and immediate impact — not just financial or reputational. Design must address emotional support, not only technical blocking.
Women Disproportionately Targeted
Both victims are female. The harassment was gendered — using sexual language to silence women who express public opinions. This pattern is structural, not incidental.
Law Can Act — but Slowly
The Renu Desai case shows the law can succeed. But it required evidence collection, formal complaints, and forensic investigation — a process far too slow to prevent harm in the moment.
Education Gaps at Every Level
In Shanaya Khan's case, schools were running emergency workshops post-incident. Prevention through education must come before, not after, a crisis.
