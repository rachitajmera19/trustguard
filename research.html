Analysis · Findings

Research Insights

Quantitative and qualitative findings from our primary research — the data and thematic patterns that informed every design and strategy decision throughout this project.

By the Numbers

Key research statistics

90%
Direct or indirect exposure rate
9 out of 10 participants had either directly experienced or witnessed cyberbullying or digital fraud in their close circle.
70%
Did not formally report
Most chose not to report due to lack of knowledge, distrust in authorities, emotional exhaustion, shame, or belief their case was too small.
100%
Found school education inadequate
Every single participant — regardless of age or background — agreed that schools and colleges fail to teach practical, effective digital safety skills.
2
Universal root causes identified
Digital literacy deficits and psychological manipulation (urgency + authority impersonation) were cited across all 10 interviews as why people fall for scams.
₹1L+
Maximum single-incident loss documented
One participant described a fake customer-support call that drained over ₹1 lakh from their family account in under 30 minutes — with no freeze mechanism available.
72 hrs
Longest documented manipulation period
A "digital arrest" scam sustained psychological pressure on an elderly couple for three days — ultimately liquidating their fixed deposits with no intervention.
Thematic Analysis

Six recurring themes

T1
Trust Exploitation
Scammers consistently exploit social trust — impersonating banks, government officials, family members. Every interview mentioned a manipulation element that bypassed logical thinking.
T2
Reporting Barriers
Multiple, overlapping barriers prevent reporting: procedural complexity, shame, disbelief in outcomes, fear of escalation. Most barriers are emotional, not technical.
T3
Platform Inadequacy
Platforms are seen as unresponsive, slow, and ineffective. The Instagram case (seller blocked after payment) illustrates universal frustration with platform moderation.
T4
Differential Vulnerability
Elderly users, women, and teenagers face distinct, heightened risks. Design solutions that treat all users as equally capable miss the most vulnerable populations entirely.
T5
Education Failure
Self-taught digital safety through YouTube, personal experience, and peer learning. Formal institutions (schools, universities) were universally rated inadequate.
T6
Growing Awareness — Uneven Distribution
Urban, younger participants showed growing digital safety awareness. But this knowledge does not reach rural areas, elderly populations, or low-literacy users.

Participant Profile
Age Range
Majority 18–30 years. Includes 1 elderly proxy (Navneet reporting father's experience). Representative of urban digital India.
Professional Background
Law students, CS students, general public, and working professionals. Intentionally diverse to capture varying digital literacy levels.
Interview Format
9 conducted in-person or via audio (transcribed by TurboScribe). 1 written submission accepted. All analysed thematically.
Geographic Coverage
Participants across India. Urban skew — a deliberate starting point given this is exploratory qualitative research. Rural users identified as a critical gap for future work.
Limitation & Next Steps
Sample is urban-skewed and relatively tech-literate. Future research must include rural users, elderly populations, and non-English speakers — the most vulnerable segments.
